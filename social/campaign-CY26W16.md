Last week in New York I figured out why I keep publishing small imperfect tools on GitHub even when there are already a dozen similar projects out there.

I built a "second brain" on a flight: an agent that ingests articles, builds a wiki, and proposes connections I'd never see on my own. Inspired by Karpathy's gist, shaped around the way I actually think. I used it for ten days across New York: it linked the Bushwick murals to Basquiat at the MoMA, Harlem gospel to Greenwich Village jazz, the Central Park Reservoir to the wooden water towers that still sit on Brooklyn rooftops. Cross-references a human brain does poorly past the first twenty sources.

There's no shortage of similar systems on GitHub. Two minutes of searching and you'll find ten. Maybe there is no space for this to become a packaged product. That doesn't matter.

Open source in its essence is shared research. You publish what you found not to win users but because someone else is thinking about the same problem. A packaged product gives you a finished solution, but it takes away the research part, the part where you also learn something by doing it.

My version: https://github.com/maeste/my-2nd-brain

If you build yours, tell me what you put in that's different from mine. That diversity is exactly the point.

I dieci giorni a New York con il mio agente "secondo cervello" erano solo un test. Il vero motivo per cui l'ho costruito è la ricerca.

Il viaggio è stato divertente: l'agente ha collegato i murales di Bushwick a Basquiat al MoMA, il gospel di Harlem al jazz del Greenwich Village, un acquedotto del 1842 alle water towers di legno ancora oggi sui tetti di Brooklyn. Connessioni che il cervello umano fa male dopo le prime venti fonti.

Ma il viaggio non era l'obiettivo. Era il banco di prova di un sistema che sto usando adesso su temi molto più seri: architetture agentiche, sistemi di memoria, i paper di ricerca che leggo ogni settimana. Quando dai in pasto all'agente un nuovo articolo a fronte di una wiki già popolata, lui propone collegamenti che non vedevi, perché "ricorda" tutte le fonti insieme nel suo contesto. È l'effetto compounding di cui parla Karpathy: ogni fonte nuova rende il sistema più prezioso, invece di disperdersi in una chat che chiuderai e dimenticherai.

Ho smesso di chiedere agli LLM "rispondimi su X". Ho cominciato a chiedergli "aggiorna quello che sai di X con questa nuova fonte, poi rispondimi". Differenza piccola nel prompt, enorme nella pratica.

The ten days I spent in New York with a "second brain" agent were just a test. The real reason I built it is research.

The trip was entertaining: the agent linked Bushwick street art to Basquiat at the MoMA, Harlem gospel to Greenwich Village jazz, a 19th-century aqueduct to the wooden water towers that still sit on Brooklyn rooftops. Cross-references a human brain does poorly past the first twenty sources.

But the trip was never the goal. It was a concrete test of a system I'm now running on much more serious topics: agent architectures, memory systems, the research papers I read every week. When you ingest a new article against an already populated wiki, the agent proposes connections you hadn't seen, because it remembers all your sources together in its context. That's the compounding effect Karpathy talks about. Every new source makes the system more valuable, instead of getting lost in a chat you'll close and forget.

I stopped asking LLMs "answer me about X". I started asking "update what you know about X with this new source, then answer me". Tiny change in the prompt, huge change in practice.

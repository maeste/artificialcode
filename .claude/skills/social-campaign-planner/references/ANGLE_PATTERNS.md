# Angle Patterns

A library of narrative angle patterns to consider during Step 2. Not every pattern fits every campaign. Use the brief's objective, audience, and core message to select 3-5 that are genuinely distinct.

## Pattern: Contrarian claim

"Everyone thinks X. Here's why X is wrong."

- Works for: thought leadership, category-creation
- Risks: sounds performative if the contrarian take is weak
- Example: "The attack surface is you, not your software."

## Pattern: Personal origin story

"A few months ago I was frustrated by X. Here's what I built."

- Works for: product launches with authentic backstory
- Risks: too inward-looking if audience does not share the pain
- Example: Voxxed Day conference to LINCE project

## Pattern: Concrete data / benchmark

"Here are the numbers. What they mean."

- Works for: technical audiences, decision-makers
- Risks: dry if numbers are not surprising
- Example: "Sweet spot is 3-5 agents in parallel. Not more. Here's why."

## Pattern: How it works

"Let me explain the architecture / the decision / the trade-off."

- Works for: developer audiences, technical teardowns
- Risks: too long for platforms that reward brevity
- Example: "Kernel-level sandboxing: bubblewrap, nono, and why we chose both"

## Pattern: User-you-are-doing-it-wrong

"If you do X, you are exposing yourself to Y."

- Works for: awareness-raising, security topics
- Risks: can sound preachy
- Example: "Clicking 'approve' 200 times a day is not security. It's fatigue."

## Pattern: Comparison / alternative

"Most people use X. Here's Y, which is better in this specific case."

- Works for: positioning against incumbents
- Risks: requires genuine differentiation
- Example: "Cloud sandboxes are fine. Local sandboxes are what you need when..."

## Pattern: Open invitation

"We built this. It is open source. Tell us what is broken."

- Works for: community building around open-source projects
- Risks: generic if overused
- Example: launch post with explicit call for PRs and issues

## Pattern: Timely hook

"Just happened. Here is what it means for you."

- Works for: reacting to news, piggy-backing on trends
- Risks: stale fast, requires real-time execution
- Example: "Anthropic blocked subscription tokens yesterday. Here is why vendor independence matters."

## Pattern: Behind the scenes

"This is how we actually work day-to-day."

- Works for: humanizing a brand, showing authenticity
- Risks: too mundane without narrative
- Example: "Running 5 agents while writing this newsletter: a day in our terminal."

## Pattern: Specific feature deep-dive

"Let me zoom into this one feature."

- Works for: breaking down a complex product into digestible stories
- Risks: requires enough substance to stand alone
- Example: "VoxCode: voice input that works on Linux"

## Pattern: Lesson learned

"After X weeks / months / attempts, here is what we know now."

- Works for: retrospectives, iteration stories
- Risks: feels like filler if the lesson is trivial
- Example: "Two weeks after launch, the feature people want most is not what we expected"

## Selection heuristics

For a campaign covering a complex product (like a software tool with multiple features), a good mix is:
- 1 personal origin story (to humanize)
- 1-2 technical deep-dives (to demonstrate substance)
- 1 contrarian claim (to signal strong POV)
- 1 open invitation (to drive CTA)

For a campaign around a single event or news:
- 1 timely hook (for reach)
- 1 concrete data (for substance)
- 1 personal take (for distinctiveness)

For thought-leadership without product launch:
- 1 contrarian claim
- 1 behind-the-scenes
- 1 how-it-works

Always ask the user which patterns resonate before generating alternatives.
